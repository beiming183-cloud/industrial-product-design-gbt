# Form Language and Mechanical Product Aesthetics

Read this file for form direction, appearance review, CMF, rendering, or extracting design language from references.

## Translate Intent into Visual Rules

Start with one sentence describing the product's intended role, such as "a quiet, precise, trustworthy desktop tool." Translate every abstract adjective into observable rules:

- proportion: slender or compact, low-center or floating, top-heavy or grounded;
- massing: monolithic, nested, interlocked, bridged, layered, or framed;
- geometry: orthogonal, cylindrical, tapered, soft-rectangular, tensioned-surface, or hybrid;
- line and surface: continuous control line, break, fold, cut, wrap, or island;
- boundary: seam, step, shadow gap, frame, chamfer, radius, or soft-hard transition;
- detail: density, scale hierarchy, rhythm, fastener visibility, and degree of technical expression;
- CMF: material honesty, reflection, touch, durability, cleanability, temperature feel, and functional zoning.

Require at least two observable rules for each adjective. Do not use untranslatable adjectives as review criteria.

## Form Hierarchy

Organize the product at three reading distances:

- `primary mass`: defines category, posture, proportion, and recognition from far away;
- `secondary mass`: explains function, support, motion, grip, and major zones;
- `tertiary detail`: resolves interfaces, seams, texture, graphics, fasteners, and touchpoints.

Resolve primary and secondary form before tertiary detail. Detail cannot repair a weak silhouette or posture.

## Proportion and Silhouette

- Discuss ratio families: overall length, width, height; body to base; solid to void; top to bottom; front to rear.
- Check positive and negative silhouette, including openings, channels, undercuts, and shadow voids.
- Align visual center of gravity with physical support, action direction, and product character.
- Evaluate at real use height, distance, and context, not only isolated perspective renders.
- Compare versions with stable views and focal length so camera changes do not masquerade as improvement.

## Geometry and Transition Grammar

- Select a limited vocabulary, such as one radius family, shared tapers, common axes, or repeated sections.
- Classify each line as functional transition, assembly boundary, or intentional graphic feature. Remove or redesign unexplained lines.
- Declare surface continuity intent: positional, tangent, or curvature. Do not use attractive highlights as a substitute for curvature evidence.
- Make openings, ribs, textures, fasteners, and patterns obey common datums, pitch, and boundary rules.
- Allow intentional contrast, but choose a protagonist; do not make every region compete for attention.

## Splits, Interfaces, and Details

- Place split lines on real assembly, material, process, or service logic and control how they cross important appearance surfaces.
- Integrate ports, buttons, handles, feet, vents, and fasteners into the mass or boundary system.
- Check mating direction, finger and tool space, visibility, feedback, misoperation, and cable bend. Appearance must not block use.
- Choose a dominant strategy of revealing technology or concealing technology; avoid accidental mixtures.
- Design seams and steps for production variation and highlight control, not zero-gap ideal renders.

## CMF

Resolve function and perception before color selection:

- Confirm material suitability for loads, heat, wear, cleaning, environment, and process.
- Use gloss, texture, and color to organize hierarchy, touchpoints, hazards, and operable areas.
- Consider temperature feel, friction, dirt, fingerprints, aging, and scratches at contact zones.
- Align material boundaries with real splits and manufacturing.
- Treat screen renders as intent only; confirm release choices with physical samples under controlled light.

## 360-Degree Coherence

Review front, rear, both sides, top, bottom, and normal operation views:

- control lines terminate or transfer deliberately;
- mass hierarchy survives every view;
- rear, underside, ports, and service areas are not leftover space;
- seams, openings, feet, cables, and labels share one grammar;
- open, closed, accessory, and motion states still read as the same product.

## Review Evidence

Use paired versions with common camera, light, and material. Record:

- target design intent;
- observed facts;
- consistency or conflict with established rules;
- explicit user preference;
- agent inference and confidence;
- functional, process, or cost effects;
- the next verifiable change.

Do not report a fake objective beauty score. When preferences conflict, state the tradeoff and recommendation.
